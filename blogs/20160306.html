Drones have become ever increasingly present in the public space. There have been major concerns whether these small devices have compromised our safety forever as countries scramble to develop regulations around these them. Drones however have the capacity for great good and it is this we will begin to explore their fire fighting capabilities.

The idea is to have a drone guard the perimeter of a fire prone area which will land and automatically recharge and take flight once more. A drone offers a vantage point where satellite view is impeded by cloud cover and provides an inexpensive real time update of ground conditions to aid fire fighters in their constant battle.

The drone will be equipped with a thermal imaging camera to help identify both the intensity and nature of a fire. With a thermal imaging camera it will be possible for the drone to determine the nature of the fire such as if it were an intense bush fire or someone illegally burning leaves in their backyard. The drone will have a guided flight path via GPS from recharge zone to recharge zone and with adaptive image processing, snap a picture of a potential fire and send it back to emergency HQ for further analysis.

With sufficient development the drone will be ideally autonomous and with the equipped thermal imaging camera be able to correctly identify real fires.

Prototyping

To simulate the thermal imaging camera, we use a large red object and the Parrot AR Drone's native front view camera. Both the image processing (OpenCV) and drone sdk have been written in node JS. As visible in the above youtube video, the proof of concept is to get the drone to hone in on our large red umbrella.We use openCV to identify a range of red and create contour lines around them, in the video there is clearly other sources of red. Via openCV we tell the drone to seek the largest contour area which it has identified as the umbrella and will ignore the other red objects. This is to show the drone will have noise tolerance to heat sources that it will likely encounter along its flight path.

Since openCV processes a stream of images, the team was tasked to create a socket that was constantly refreshed to show a video stream of images for use during the presentation.
